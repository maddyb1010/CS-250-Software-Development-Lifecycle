## Reflection

### How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating “user stories” help with this?

To interpret user needs, I focus on understanding what the user is actually trying to accomplish instead of just listing features. In this course, user stories helped translate those needs into something actionable by framing requirements from the user’s perspective. For example, in the SNHU Travel project, a story like wanting customized destination recommendations clearly defined both the goal and the value to the user, which made it easier to design and test the feature.

User stories also make it easier to prioritize work because they connect directly to user value. Instead of guessing what to build first, the team can focus on the highest-priority needs in the backlog and adjust as requirements change. This reflects Agile practices where work is continuously reprioritized and refined throughout development.

### How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?

I approach development as an iterative process rather than trying to plan everything upfront. This course reinforced that requirements are not always fully known at the beginning, so it is more effective to build in small increments, get feedback, and improve over time.

In the future, I would want to incorporate practices like continuous integration and test-driven development. Continuous integration allows teams to frequently combine and test code, which helps catch issues early and maintain a working product throughout development. Test-driven development supports this by focusing on writing tests first and building only what is needed to pass them, which improves code quality and keeps development focused on requirements. I would also continue using retrospectives because they allow teams to reflect and improve after each iteration.

### What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?

Being a good team member means actively contributing to both the work and the communication within the team. In Agile, development is not done in isolated roles. Instead, teams are cross-functional and responsible for the overall quality of the product, not just individual tasks.

This means collaborating with others, being open to feedback, and helping solve problems as a group. Strong communication is also important because Agile teams rely on transparency and constant updates to stay aligned. Practices like daily standups and shared progress tracking help ensure that everyone understands what is being worked on and where issues exist. Overall, a good team member is someone who is reliable, communicates clearly, and focuses on the success of the team rather than just their own work.
